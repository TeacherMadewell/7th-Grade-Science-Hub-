Your lab data is in — so what does it actually prove about the force?

What do you NOTICE? What do you WONDER?

Today's question

A guess says 'I think charge matters.' A conclusion says 'Here are three trials that show it.' Which scientist are you?

Today you willAnalyze my results from the prior day's activity and draw an evidence-backed conclusion about which factors change the electric force, and by how much.
You've got it when
  • I can read a trend off a data table and name what it shows
  • I can state whether charge or distance made the force stronger or weaker, with numbers to back it up
  • I can write a one-sentence conclusion that uses evidence from my data
I do

A conclusion = a claim + evidence from your data. When charge goes up, force goes up. When distance goes up, force goes down.

Check before the sim · click to reveal
  • A data table shows: small charge → 2 N; medium charge → 5 N; large charge → 9 N. What trend does this show?→ as charge increases, force increases — Each row has more charge and a bigger force number — the data goes UP together, so charge and force rise together.
  • A data table shows: 1 cm apart → 8 N; 3 cm apart → 3 N; 5 cm apart → 1 N. What trend does this show?→ as distance increases, force decreases — Each row has more distance and a smaller force number — as the gap grows, the force shrinks. Distance and force move in opposite directions.
  • A conclusion that says 'I think charge matters' is stronger than one that says 'When charge doubled, force doubled in all 4 trials.'→ false — Guesses don't win arguments — specific numbers from your own trials do. The second sentence is a real evidence-backed conclusion.

Now you know the two dials — let's turn them in the sim.

We do — Turn the Dials · predict BEFORE I drag
  • You run the sim with charge = 1 μC and distance = 2 m. Force reads 2.2 N. You then increase charge to 2 μC (same distance). The data says force will be…
  • Sim trial: distance = 1 m → force = 9.0 N; distance = 2 m → force = 2.2 N; distance = 3 m → force = 1.0 N. The data shows that doubling the distance…
  • In your sim results, every time you moved the charges closer, the force reading went UP. That is evidence that distance makes force…
  • You triple the charge while keeping distance the same. Based on the data pattern, the force should…
  • If the sim shows force = 4 N at 2 m and force = 1 N at 4 m, that data supports the claim that distance and force move in OPPOSITE directions.
